# Modern Voice Guide for Roman Letter Translations

## The Goal
Any English speaker should be able to pick up one of these letters and immediately follow what's being said — who's writing, what they're talking about, and why it matters. The tone should feel like well-written nonfiction: clear, engaging, and respectful of both the reader and the original author.

## The Register
Think of how a good historian writes for a general audience — someone like Mary Beard, Tom Holland, or Patrick Wyman himself. It's literate, confident prose that doesn't talk down to people but also never makes them reach for a dictionary. These letters were written by serious, often brilliant people. The translations should sound like serious, intelligent people writing today.

## What "Modern English" Means Here

NOT this (19th-century translation, sounds archaic):
> "I must beseech you, Most Blessed Father, that the joy we received through you by God's bounty may be preserved for us by your prayers."

NOT this (overcorrected, too casual):
> "Hey Father, keep praying for us okay? We need the luck to hold."

YES this (clear, natural, literate):
> "Most Blessed Father, I ask you to keep us in your prayers. The blessings God has granted us through you — we need them to endure."

## Guidelines

### 1. Use clear, modern vocabulary
- Replace genuinely archaic words: "beseech" → "beg" or "ask earnestly"; "wherefore" → "for this reason"
- But don't strip away all formality — "I implore you" is perfectly good modern English
- Technical terms are fine with a brief context note: "the pallium [a wool vestment signifying a bishop's authority]"
- Latin administrative terms that have no English equivalent should be explained once, then used naturally

### 2. Honorifics are fine
- "Your Holiness", "Most Blessed Father", "Your Excellency" — these are normal English and immediately understood
- Just trim excessive stacking: "my most venerable and holy lord and brother, worthy of all esteem in Christ" → "my dear brother" or "my venerable brother"
- Self-deprecating formulas like "your unworthy servant" can be simplified to "I" unless the humility is the point

### 3. Sentence clarity
- Aim for clear, well-structured sentences — not artificially short, but never requiring a second read
- Break up truly unwieldy sentences, but a well-constructed long sentence is fine
- Semicolons are allowed; just don't chain three clauses together
- The test: could you read this sentence aloud and a listener would follow it?

### 4. Prefer active voice and direct statements
- "It has been reported to us that certain irregularities have occurred" → "I've received reports of serious problems"
- But passive voice is fine when it's natural: "The letter was delivered by your deacon" is perfectly clear

### 5. Context notes
- Add brief bracketed notes for things a modern reader wouldn't know
- Keep them informative but not encyclopedic
- Example: "[The Donatists were a North African Christian faction who believed the church should be pure and refused to readmit anyone who had caved under Roman persecution]"
- Place them on first mention, then don't repeat

### 6. Let the personality come through
- Augustine: warm, searching, intellectually restless — like a brilliant friend who can't stop thinking out loud
- Jerome: sharp, learned, often cutting — the kind of writer who makes enemies because he's too witty to resist a devastating line
- Gregory the Great: clear-headed, authoritative, deeply compassionate — a natural leader handling an overwhelming workload
- Sidonius: literary, elegant, nostalgic — conscious that he's preserving a way of life that's disappearing
- Basil: practical, caring, occasionally exasperated — a born administrator with a genuine pastoral heart
- Cassiodorus: formal, deliberate, learned — crafting an image of civilized Roman governance for a barbarian court

### 7. No metadata headers
Do NOT include From/To/Date/Context headers in translations. This metadata is already displayed in the page header. Output ONLY the translated letter text — no preamble, no framing, no metadata blocks.

### 8. The readability test
A reader should never have to go back and reread a sentence. If they do, the sentence needs rewriting. But "readable" doesn't mean "simple" — it means well-crafted. These are letters from some of the most important people in Western history. The translations should do them justice.

## Examples

### Gregory the Great — administrative letter
19TH CENTURY:
> "Reports have reached me concerning some of our brothers — that certain of them, in violation of apostolic order, have been presuming to act in ways that are altogether inadmissible."

MODERN:
> "I've received reports that some of our clergy have been acting in clear violation of church rules — and what they've done is simply unacceptable."

### Jerome — being Jerome
19TH CENTURY:
> "I would not presume to attack the philosophers of the Academy, for when could the authority of such eminent men fail to move me?"

MODERN:
> "I wouldn't dream of attacking the Academic philosophers — the weight of their reputation would certainly give me pause. But I do think their views have been badly misunderstood."

### Augustine — reflecting on friendship
19TH CENTURY:
> "A certain long desire for speaking, already poured out in my mind, when I was resting in your holiness..."

MODERN:
> "I've wanted to write to you for a long time. Even just thinking about you brings a kind of peace, and I find myself eager to hear your thoughts as much as to share my own."

### Cassiodorus — Theoderic writing to the Emperor
19TH CENTURY:
> "It befits us, most merciful Emperor, to seek peace, since we are known to have no cause for anger."

MODERN:
> "It is fitting, most merciful Emperor, that we should seek peace — especially since there is no grievance between us. A ruler who comes to the table prepared for justice has already proven his case."
